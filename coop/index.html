Co-op

Companies

At least 300 companies from 58 different cities have hired an SE student. Students had the opportunity to work in many different types of environments, including working at a 5 person startup, developing in Kenya, doing research with professors, and working at a big tech company. Some students tried new positions outside of software engineering such as product designer, data scientist, associate quantitative trader, consultant, and more.

The benefit of having 6 internships is that students get to explore different company sizes doing different positions in different cities. This is invaluable for students because it helps students figure out what they want upon graduation.

Locations

91% of students interned in at least 2 different countries. One of the factors behind 9% of students only interning in Canada were restrictions from work visas which primarily affects non-Canadians.

The majority that interned in the US worked in California and specifically in Silicon Valley. One of the reasons for this is because Silicon Valley is the HQ for many large tech companies that hire software engineers such as Google, Facebook, Apple, etc.

During 5 of the 6 co-op terms, there was at least one student working outside of North America. This included cities such as Stockholm, Nairobi, Phnom Penh, Shanghai, Tokyo, etc.

Favourite Locations

31% of students that worked in California preferred working elsewhere. Although it is still preferred by the majority of students, this suggests that it is not for everyone. This suggests that the infamous 'Cali or Bust' mentality can set up a significant number of current students for disappointment compared to other opportunities.

Salary

By the last co-op term, students tripled the salary of their first internship. This is largely explained by the salary differences between different locations, extra stipends included in offers. These factors compensate for higher living costs, especially in San Francisco where housing easily costs between $2,000 to $3,000 per month.

Salary as a metric

In order to rank jobs appropriately, salary is used as a proxy metric to measure job quality. Due to the subjectiveness of this metric, the following results should be taken with a grain of salt because higher salary does not imply higher job satisfaction.

Grades

Students who started coding earlier were paid similarly compared to students who started coding later.

Years of coding experience

Students who started coding earlier were paid similarly compared to students who started coding later. It doesn't matter if a student started at the age of 13 or 18. This suggests that students that started coding later are not at a disadvantage in terms of co-op job opportunities.

Hackathons

Students who attended at least one hackathon earned 18% more on average than those that didn't attend any. In other words, students who show passion for building projects get better job opportunities than those that don't. Alternatively, this result could be because students that attended a hackathon cared more about getting a better job.

Students who attended more than 5 hackathons earned approximately 27% more than those that only attended between 1 to 5 hackathons. This could suggest that the former has a lot more relevant work to show which is more appealing to employers.

Side projects

Students that build side projects earn 13% more on average than those that don't build side projects. The more time that a student invests into side projects, the more they are likely to get paid. Side projects are often seen as the closest thing to software work experience, making it a great time investment to compensate for any lack of work experience.

It is important to mention that side projects and hackathons are not the only way to find a job. There have been many less conventional ways students have used to find job opportunities such as referrals, networking, etc.

It is just as important to note that students who are not involved in software-related extracurriculars still find great jobs. In fact, many SE students and faculty encourage pursuing interests besides software.

Admission average

Admission averages have no significant impact on early job opportunities. A 90 in one school could be harder to achieve than a 99 in another school. In other words, admission averages are an inaccurate metric to determine how successful someone is because high schools do not standardize their grades with each other.
