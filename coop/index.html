Where did students work?

Companies

With 6 available co-ops, students took full advantage and interned in all sorts of environments. For example, working at a 5 person startup, developing in Kenya, doing research with professors, TA'ing a course, etc. Some students also tried new positions outside of software engineering such as product designer, data scientist, associate quantitative trader, consulting, etc.

The benefit of having 6 internships is that students get to explore different company sizes doing different positions in different cities. This is invaluable for students because it helps us figure out what we want to do once we graduate.

Locations

91% of students interned in at least 2 different countries. Out of the 9% of students that only interned in Canada, some are restricted to working in Canada due to their Visa.

After the 5th co-op, there is a decrease in the number of students working in the US. Most people that worked in the US worked in California, specifically in Silicon Valley. This makes sense because Silicon Valley is the HQ for many large tech companies that hire software engineers such as Google, Facebook, Apple, etc.

During 5 of the 6 co-op terms, there was at least one student working outside of North America. Students worked in cities like Stockholm, Nairobi, Phnom Penh, Shanghai, Tokyo, etc.

Favourite Locations

California was not the favourite co-op location for 43% of students. Although it is still preferred by the majority of students, this suggests that it's not for everyone. This suggests that the infamous 'Cali or Bust' mentality can set up a significant number of current students for disappointment compared to other opportunities.

Does X affect job opportunities?

Salary as a metric

In order to rank jobs appropriately, salary is used as a subjective metric to measure job quality. Due to the subjectiveness of this metric, the following results should be taken with a grain of salt. This is because lower paying jobs can be enjoyed more depending on the person.

Years of coding experience

Students who started coding earlier were paid similarly compared to students who started coding later. It doesn't matter if a student started at the age of 13 or 18. This suggests that students that start coding near the start of university are not at a disadvantage in terms of co-op job hunting.
